The road through Hayden Valley empties out about an hour before sunset, and we pulled into a turnout where the river makes a slow, lazy bend toward the southeast. There were already three or four bison grazing on the far bank, and within twenty minutes a small wolf pack had drifted into the meadow behind them — too far for a photograph that would do them justice, but close enough that we could hear the calves on the wind.

We made coffee on the back step. The light went from straw to copper to that very specific color that western valleys turn for about ninety seconds before the sun drops below the rim, and then everything was blue and cold and the bison were silhouettes.

The wolves moved like a single shadow that someone had cut into pieces.

It was the kind of evening that reminds you why you keep doing this — why you put up with the propane and the dump stations and the eight-hour drives between water fills. We slept with the windows cracked. There was frost on the awning by morning.
